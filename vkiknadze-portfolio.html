The Problem

Group ordering turns match-day excitement into coordination chaos.

75% of Spanish football fans watch in groups of five or more. But the current group ordering experience makes the host the single point of failure — managing seven people's preferences across WhatsApp, making decisions alone under time pressure. The friction isn't the food. It's organizing the people.

"The biggest match-day friction isn't ordering the food — it's organizing people. And no competitor owns that moment."
The Strategic Insight

In Spain, football is a social ritual — not just a sport.

Three data points locked the strategy: 75% watch in groups · 46% at home · 67% of game-day orders placed in the T-90 "golden hour" before kickoff. The window exists. The behavior exists. What's missing is a brand that makes that moment feel like part of the game — not a logistics problem to solve before it starts.

Campaign Concept
Los goles, en el campo.
El golazo, en casa.
The goals are on the pitch. The real win is at your table.

Uber Eats becomes the teammate behind every great watch party — turning the host's stressful coordination role into a collaborative, playful ritual that brings every guest into the action before kickoff.

The Execution — Match Day Jam

A football-formation group ordering game, built inside the app.

  • 1

    WhatsApp-native sharing. Host opens a squad, friends join via a shared link. No separate app, no friction — it lives inside Uber Eats.

  • 2

    Football formation ordering. Friends pick positions (Defensa, Centrocampista, Delantero, Portero) — each position owns a part of the order. The cart fills the lineup visually, like a real team.

  • 3

    Squad-based discount unlocks. More participants = bigger rewards. Free delivery, 20% off, Uber One trial — unlocked progressively as the team grows. KPI 03 and 05 baked into the mechanic.

  • 4

    Penalty shootout finale. Alternating shots and saves decide who pays — the social, shareable emotional peak that extends the Uber Eats brand into the match-day story itself.

The Brief

Where should a premium protein brand invest in Spain right now?

YoPro sits in a market in transition: Spain's online grocery is in a 27% CAGR breakout, TikTok Shop just launched in the country, and retail media platforms are offering incremental reach that most brands haven't mapped yet. The research goal was to build a definitive channel playbook — not a media plan, but a strategic map of what each channel can and cannot do for a brand like YoPro.

"Spanish mid-tier creators currently beat macro influencers on ROI. CTV penetration reached 95%. And TikTok Shop changes TikTok's role from awareness to commerce."
Key Research Findings

Six strategic conclusions that reshape channel planning.

  • →

    Spain's online grocery sector is growing at 27% CAGR — the window for premium CPG brands to establish retail media presence is open now, not later.

  • →

    Spanish mid-tier creators (50K–500K followers) deliver higher engagement ROI than macro talent — and are dramatically more affordable.

  • →

    TikTok Shop's Spain launch transforms TikTok from a pure awareness channel into a full-funnel commerce platform — the brand strategy implications are significant.

  • →

    CTV ad spend in Spain grew +48% YoY against 95% household penetration — an underpriced channel for premium brand storytelling.

Deliverables

Research built for a planning team, not a presentation.

  • 📊

    Full 11-channel analysis with Spain-specific benchmarks: CPCs, engagement rates, conversion rates, and measurement architecture per channel.

  • 📐

    Channel-fit matrix scoring each channel on audience match, scale, conversion proximity, and measurement quality — calibrated for a premium protein brand.

  • 🎨

    Five creative mockups (Meta Reel, TikTok Spark Ad, Amazon SERP, Google SERP, Carrefour Links) illustrating format best practices per channel.

  • 🔬

    Measurement architecture covering AMC, Safe Haven, Glovo Manager, and CAPI — designed to unify cross-channel attribution for the planning team.

The Insight That Reframed the Brief
Retail media incrementality is unproven by default — not assumed. Every brand in Spain is spending on Carrefour Links and Amazon DSP without a measurement plan. That's the structural advantage available right now.

Ballantine's True Music — FY27 Communication Plan

A €1.5M, Gen Z-focused 360° communications strategy developed in a team and pitched directly to Pernod Ricard. The brief: bring Ballantine's True Music platform back to cultural relevance — reclaiming the brand's iconicity in music without rewriting what already works.

The Brief

Bring the True Music platform back to cultural relevance — without reinventing it.

Ballantine's True Music has 10 years of heritage and sits at #11 in Spain's music territory rankings — the only FMCG brand in the top 15, above festivals like BBK Live and Sónar. The equity is there. But the brief was clear: "We're not looking to rewrite it. We're looking to make it real in a way that is relevant and sustainable over time." The task was a full FY27 communication plan, Gen Z-first, €1.5M budget, across all touchpoints.

"Music is mates' no.1 passion point globally. Ballantine's True Music has 10 years of credibility — the brand isn't absent from music. It just stopped showing up in a way Gen Z actually cares about."
The Strategic Frame

From sponsor to facilitator. From presence to genuine connection.

The strategic shift the brief was calling for wasn't louder activation — it was a different role entirely. Ballantine's new mandate in music: facilitator. Not the brand that puts its logo on a stage. The brand that connects fans with artists in a way that feels true to both. The platform — "Own Your Beat" — made the brand the bridge between the audience and the music they love, with "Stay True" as the emotional permission to be yourself in those moments.

Brand Positioning Applied
"The Playfully Irreverent Friend"
Unapologetically ourselves. Quietly confident. Never needing to shout the loudest. — Ballantine's Brand Persona

The persona was the brief's most useful constraint. Every touchpoint recommendation had to pass a single test: does this feel like something the playfully irreverent friend would actually do? That ruled out logo-heavy sponsorship, celebrity-led content without substance, and anything that felt like the brand was buying relevance rather than earning it.

The Communication Plan

A 360° platform built for FY27, designed to compound over time.

  • 1

    Local brand experiences as the cultural anchor. Intimate, artist-led events in third spaces — bars, clubs, studios — that put real fans in genuine proximity to artists they love. Not ticketed concerts. Curated moments that feel earned, not bought.

  • 2

    Gen Z social strategy built around authentic expression. Platform-native content that lets the audience do their thing — not a brand directing what "staying true" looks like, but giving them the stages and tools to show it themselves.

  • 3

    Influencer strategy rooted in shared values, not budget. True partnerships built on mutual authenticity — creators whose relationship to music is real, not managed. The brand backs them, not the other way around.

  • 4

    Festival presence redesigned for fan-first impact. Less logo on a banner, more branded moments that pull fans closer to the music — backstage access, artist meetups, genuine experiences the brand makes possible.

  • 5

    On- and off-trade activation tied to the music territory. In-bar moments and retail assets that carry the True Music platform into the purchase environment — not a discount mechanic, a cultural signal.

Starbucks Spain — Making Growth Real

A €1M comms strategy developed in a team and pitched directly to Dentsu Spain on behalf of Alsea. The brief exposed a deceptively healthy business: revenue growing +6.1%, but driven by store openings — not by people choosing Starbucks more often. The real challenge was unlocking same-store transaction growth.

The Brief

Revenue growth that hides a stagnation problem.

Starbucks Spain (operated by Alsea) was growing — but the headline number was flattering. Revenue up +6.1%, store count up +8.5%. Strip out the new openings and same-store transaction growth was barely moving at +1.2% CAGR. Dentsu's brief was clear: find a way to drive comparable store performance. Not just open more locations and call it growth. The real measure of health was getting people to choose Starbucks more often, in the stores that already exist.

"Spain's chained foodservice market is the least developed in Europe — only 12% of total. Every other major market has been won by chains. The opportunity isn't niche. It's the whole category."
The Strategic Tension

Three platforms. One brand. Which direction?

The brief created a three-way strategic fork. Option one: stay with "Despierta con Starbucks" — the current local platform targeting 18-25 year-olds on indulgent beverages. It's performing but it's narrow. Option two: adapt the US "Back to Starbucks / Hello Again" global reset, which recentres the brand on its coffee credentials and "third place" roots. Option three: develop a new direction entirely, built for Spain's specific market opportunity. The team's challenge was to take a position and defend it against Dentsu's strategists.

Strategic Direction
"The Routine Worth Returning To"
Shift Starbucks' relevance from indulgent treat to the weekday morning ritual — targeting urban professionals aged 25-40 who've never made Starbucks part of their routine, in a market where chain coffee has barely been tried.
The Consumer Pivot

From Gen Z treat to urban professional ritual.

The current target — 18-25, special occasion, indulgent format — represents a ceiling, not a floor. The growth opportunity sits with older urban consumers: 25-40, weekday mornings, routine-driven, making a deliberate choice about where to have their first coffee of the day. This audience is underserved by Starbucks' current comms. They're the ones whose frequency of visit is the variable that moves same-store transaction numbers. The platform had to speak to them without abandoning what made Starbucks a cultural object in the first place — the space, the ritual, the sense of being somewhere intentional.

Execution Framework
  • 1

    Platform decision — adapt "Hello Again" for Spain. The global reset gave the brand permission to recentre on coffee craft and the third-place proposition. Rather than create a parallel local identity, the team argued for coherence: one brand story, locally executed with Spanish consumer truths.

  • 2

    Morning routine conquest campaign. A media strategy built around the morning commute window — CTV pre-roll, OOH near transit, digital audio (Spotify morning moments), and in-app retargeting — timed to intercept the weekday coffee decision before it defaults to a competitor or the office machine.

  • 3

    Barista-led content as a trust signal. Starbucks' differentiator versus craft independents and convenience chains isn't just the drink — it's the barista relationship. Content strategy leaned into authentic store-level storytelling, positioning the barista as a character in the customer's morning, not just a transaction point.

  • 4

    Loyalty mechanic to convert trial into frequency. A structured CRM and loyalty push targeting lapsed and low-frequency visitors, using the Starbucks app to rebuild habitual behaviour through personalised morning offers — the lever most directly tied to same-store transaction growth.

Philosophy

The brands that win aren't the ones with the biggest budgets. They're the ones that understood something true about their audience before anyone else did.

I'm at the start of my career — but I've worked hard to earn the right to have a point of view. Everything in this portfolio came from taking real briefs seriously, asking uncomfortable questions, and being willing to defend a position in front of people who know the business better than I do.

01

Start with the behavior, not the idea

Every brief I've worked on, I've tried to find the behavioral truth before touching the campaign. What are people actually doing? What sits between them and what the brand wants? The idea comes after.

02

Being from three places is an advantage

Growing up in Georgia, working in Russia, studying in Spain — I don't have one cultural reference point for what "normal" consumer behavior looks like. That makes it easier to see what's actually specific to a market, versus what everyone assumes is universal.

03

Be honest about what you don't know

I'm at the beginning of my career and I know it. The most useful thing I can offer isn't experience — it's genuine curiosity, commercial awareness from my time at Werfen, and a willingness to do the work properly from the start.
